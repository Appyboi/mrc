The Ideological Illusions of Patriarchy: Masculinity and Femininity

Introduction:

Strength, Weakness, Oppression and Tell-Tales

“One is not born, but rather becomes, woman.”

– Simone de Beauvoir, The Second Sex1

“Sexual attributes are a biological given, but gender is a product of historical process. The fact that women bear children is due to sex; that women nurture children is due to gender, a cultural construct. It is gender which has been chiefly responsible for fixing women’s place in society.” [Italics by M.]

– Gerda Lerner, The Creation of Patriarchy2

What is a woman? What is a man? If gender is different from biological sex, and woman is not always female, and man is not always male; then why are people described in terms of gender as cisgender and transgender based on their conformance or non-conformance with their biological sex assigned at birth? If biological sex is not all that is there to one’s gender, then what else is there? I contend that it is masculinity and femininity that make cisgender men out of males and cisgender women out of females, and it is also masculinity and femininity whose rejection makes transgender people out of males and females. The question then, is, what are masculinity and femininity? Do they have a basis in nature or are they pure constructs of social consciousness? Would men and women conform to masculinity and femininity even if they were not as strictly reinforced in society and culture as they are? Consequently, would there be transgender people if masculinity and femininity were not as strictly reinforced? I aim to perform a Marxist epistemological analysis on gender to show that masculinity and femininity are ideological illusions of patriarchy that falsely attribute ideas of physical strength as well as political power with men, and those of physical weakness and political subservience with women. I take, for my basis of understanding Marxist epistemology, The Theory of Knowledge by Com. Maurice Cornforth, where he elucidates the Marxist understanding of epistemology by undertaking a dialectical and historical materialist analysis of knowledge. Furthermore, I aim to elaborate on some aspects of the performance of femininity and of women’s and transgender people’s oppression, understanding their oppression and emancipation from a Marxist-Feminist perspective.

All across class society one finds ideas about masculinity and femininity to assign gender roles to people not just in social production but also in social customs, religious traditions, mythological stories, literary prose and poetry, etc. It is not uncommon to come across ideas around masculinity and femininity that establish men as strong and powerful, and women as weak and subservient. One can take, for an example, the short stories of the prolific Danish writer of children’s fairy tales in the 19th Century, Hans Christian Anderson, that often show the suffering of women and girls whether through sacrifice or through punishment. An example of suffering through sacrifice can be found in his The Little Mermaid where a young mermaid exchanges her very enchanting voice with human legs even when warned in advance that her feet will bleed terribly as if she is walking on sharp knives; all of this just to come closer to a human boy she falls in love with at first sight and has not even interacted with before, and to get an immortal human soul. Even after all this sacrifice, the boy who is a prince makes her his treasured sexual slave or concubine, and gets married to someone else while she becomes an angelic nun who must perform good deeds for three hundred years to get an immortal soul. Similarly, we can take an example of suffering through punishment in The Red Shoes wherein a spoiled little girl wears red instead of black shoes to a church twice and is thus punished that her shoes will never come off when she dances, and she ends up getting her feet amputated; and later prays to God that she dies and her suffering ends in heaven (and God grants her her wish). A story by Hans Christian Anderson that particularly interests our subject, however, is The Princess and the Pea, wherein a prince of a marriageable age faces difficulty finding a suitable bride for himself as he is not sure if the princesses he has been meeting are “real” princesses or not. One stormy night, a young woman seeks refuge in his castle, claiming to be a princess as well; but the queen must test her guest to determine her “realness” as a princess. Hence the queen grants a bed covered by twenty mattresses and twenty featherbeds stacked on top of one another for the alleged princess to sleep on. Herein she adds a twist: a hard, uncooked pea below the first mattress. In the morning, the young woman confesses that she could not sleep at all, as there was something in the bed that made her uncomfortable and restless. This makes the queen and the prince assured that she is, in fact, a real princess; because a princess must be delicate enough to be discomforted by a tiny pea even when a score of mattresses and featherbeds lie atop. The story ends with the prince and princess getting married, thereby making it convenient for the author to avoid showing that bizarre situation wherein this flower-like delicate woman is supposed to endure the pain of childbirth and deliver a healthy child as per royal standards. Therefore, this story’s message that a woman is supposed to be delicate (physically weak, feminine) is a paradoxical idea because one needs lots of physical strength to fulfil what the same patriarchal people call the “natural function of women”, viz. child-bearing.

How are women supposed to be both delicate and strong at the same time? Why do there lie such contradictory ideas in the conceptions of masculinity and femininity? I contend that masculinity and femininity can best be understood as ideological illusions that maintain patriarchy in class society. Consequently, contradictory ideas are a feature of their illusory characteristics.

Summarizing Marxist Epistemology

Before we delve further into how patriarchy operates in the base and the superstructure as well as how masculinity and femininity are ideological illusions of patriarchy, it is essential for us to understand the concept of ideology and ideological illusions in Marxist epistemology. I will briefly summarize what constitutes ideology and ideological illusions as per the British Marxist philosopher Com. Maurice Cornforth’s The Theory of Knowledge where he explains Marxist epistemology by performing a dialectical and historical materialist analysis of knowledge.

The summary can be stated as follows:

As dialectical materialism holds, matter or external reality exists independently of our mind or the consciousness of matter, and that matter is primary while thought is secondary. It follows that consciousness is the reflection of matter in the life-process of the human brain. It also follows that consciousness passes from lower stages of sensuous-perceptual consciousness regarding our immediate surroundings to higher stages wherein we establish the need to interact with fellow human beings, and thus we develop language, thought, logic as well as abstract ideas. An example of abstract ideas provided by Cornforth is the idea of land ownership — land is a concrete idea that corresponds to a directly perceptible object that we can point at, but its ownership is an abstract idea that we can not relate to a directly perceptible object. Nonetheless,

“[...] these abstract ideas are the ideal reflection of something real and objective — the production relations established at a definite stage of the evolution of social production.”

– Maurice Cornforth, The Theory of Knowledge

The development of abstract ideas leads to the formation of mental or intellectual labour as a distinct category from material labour. With the widening of the gap between mental and material labour, between theoretical and practical activity; thinking

“becomes the special province of mental as distinct from material labour, all the more does it cut loose from the practice and the experiences of ordinary working life. It becomes free to elaborate all manner of general concepts and general views about the world and about society. What we think becomes distinct from what we experience or perceive.”

– Maurice Cornforth, The Theory of Knowledge

And this is where Marx and Engels write,

“From this moment onwards consciousness can really flatter itself that it is something other than consciousness of existing practice, that it really represents something without representing something real; from now on consciousness is in a position to emancipate itself from the world and to proceed to the formation of ‘pure’ theory, theology, philosophy, ethics, etc.”

– Marx and Engels, The German Ideology

This means that in the separation of mental and material labour, thinking or developing ideas becomes a task of people with specialized occupations around mental labour such as priests, chiefs or leaders, philosophers, and later scientists. At first, consciousness transcends from the state of sensing and perceiving objects in the immediate external environment of a being, to a state of thinking about abstract concepts or developing abstract ideas. Only then does consciousness or thinking become “pure” consciousness such as philosophy, ethics, theology, etc. that are separated from the material process of social production.

As “pure consciousness” develops more, there come into being social ideas believed by a set of people in different epochs, such as the idea of legal rights where in the slave society, slaves had no rights; in feudal society, serfs had some rights but visibly much lesser than those of feudal lords; and in capitalist society there are “human rights” applicable to all humans on-paper — albeit not actualizable in reality given that capitalism is a stage of class society too and thus has to exploit and oppress a large majority of people to sustain itself. These abstract ideas around legal rights explain the views of definite groups in definite epochs on the said topic — in this case these are the views of the ruling classes of specific stages of historical development of class society. Similarly, there are also definite views by working classes and other progressive sections of the society. For instance, under capitalism, the proletariat may resist the idea of mere on-paper legal rights and fight for total economic and political emancipation of the proletariat as a class.

“Such more or less systematic views, which are historically-evolved by definite social groups in definite stages of social development, and which vary according to their social origin, are called ideologies. And the development of such views is called ideological development.”

– Maurice Cornforth, The Theory of Knowledge

Ideologies are not products of individuals’ brains but rather a product of the social conditions that give birth to these ideas in people’s brains and make them popular or unpopular. Since humanity has been living in class society for centuries, ideologies have a class character, e.g. communist ideology appeals to working classes, liberal ideology appeals to the middle classes; proletarian feminism appeals to working classes, liberal feminism appeals to bourgeois and middle classes, etc.

Moreover, ideologies are a reflection of material reality, but they may not necessarily be true reflections. A bourgeois feminist like Sheryl Sandberg may reflect from material reality and advocate for an increase in the number of women in leadership roles in companies like Facebook. She may genuinely believe in the cause of feminism (the way she knows it) and of women empowerment, and may even be acting out of genuine concern and sympathy for fellow women. However, with her worldview being limited by her upbringing and experiences as a rich woman, she does not realize that mere women empowerment and promotion into leadership roles in companies will not solve the issue of millions of poor working class women to whom, say a company like Apple or Nike “outsources” and “offshores” their manual labour requiring tasks: with unsafe working conditions, long work hours and very less pay. She has been conditioned by a bourgeois ideology all her life where she only extends sympathy to women of bourgeois and petty-bourgeois classes. At the same time, her idea of promoting women in leadership roles in multinational companies in the name of feminism serves the class interests of the capitalist class in general, because instead of encouraging women to fight class exploitation and patriarchal oppression, she encourages women to join the labour aristocracy and work on behalf of the capitalists to exploit other workingpeople. Hence, her ideology is a bourgeois ideology and the sham of women empowerment she believes in is an illusory idea.

In reality, fragments of science or truth as well as of illusions co-exist in ideologies. In Sheryl Sandberg’s case, her concern and passion for women’s rights comes from a place of truth that women are indeed suppressed in the corporate sector. However, her understanding of the roots and solution of the issue are both illusory as discussed above. As Com. Maurice Cornforth clarifies,

“[...] the opposition and interpenetration of scientific and illusory elements in ideology cannot be conceived so simply, as if ideas about one thing were scientific while ideas about some other thing were illusory. The fact is rather that scientific and illusory elements oppose each other and interpenetrate in the ideas formed about one and the same thing.” [Italics by M.]

Moreover, illusory ideas or ideological illusions are bound to arise from bourgeois ideologies as capitalism is a reactionary system today, incompatible with the rise in development of productive forces.

Ideological illusions are not simple errors, as Maurice Cornforth explains by exemplifying that if somebody makes an error in computing or remembering the square of thirteen, like 132 = 166 instead of 132 = 169, then that is a simple error of calculating or memorizing incorrectly. However, if they say that thirteen is an unlucky number, then they are in an ideological illusion where they are applying preconceived ideas of luck onto numbers in an uncritical manner even if these ideas have a definite source in experience and practice. As he further explains,

“Similarly, if someone says that the British Constitution was introduced into Parliament by Oliver Cromwell, this is an erroneous statement, arising from an insufficient study of British constitutional history. But suppose [they say] that the British Constitution is an expression of the unique genius of the Anglo-Saxon race, or is God’s gift to the British people. These statements, though also erroneous, are not simply errors in history. They arise from applying to social affairs preconceived ideas about racial genius or God.

Thus [ideological] illusions constitute a special kind of error, arising from a quite definite mode of misconceiving things in terms of preconceived ideas.”

– Maurice Cornforth, The Theory of Knowledge
