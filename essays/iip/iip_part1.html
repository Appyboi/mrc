The Ideological Illusions of Patriarchy: Masculinity and Femininity

Part One:

Ideological Illusions and Gender

Let us now explore the five principal features of ideological illusions in class-divided society as characterized by Com. Maurice Cornforth by quoting his writings; and simultaneously understand how they are applicable to masculinity and femininity.

(A) Ideological Reflection of Production Relations

TRIGGER WARNING: Mention of r*pe

[Concept] “The first feature of ideological illusions is that they always arise as reflections of particular, historically constituted relations of production. Their source is the production relations of society.[…]

Consciousness is never anything but a reflection of material existence. First there is matter, objective being, and then, secondarily, there is consciousness, the reflection of matter. The mind has no inner sources of its own, from which ideas can be derived. Every idea, every element of ideology, is derived from and reflects some objective reality, some real aspect of the material world.

The source of the illusions in ideology is always the real economic structure of society. As [humans] live, so do they think. Corresponding to the relations they enter into in producing the means of life, they produce social ideas and social theories.

[Example] The supernatural world which [people] conjure up for themselves in their ideas is never, in the last analysis, anything other than a reflection of the real world of society, of the social relations within which [humans] live their earthly lives. The world of the supernatural always serves as the guardian of the basic fabric of society. The tribal religion stands guard over the tribe and protects tribal relations, just as the ideas of Christianity today have been so adapted that heaven seems to stand guard over the bourgeois order of society. The supernatural world which guards and justifies the social order is created in the image of that social order.” [Italics by M.]

For patriarchy, understanding this ideological illusion would also answer another question of our interest, viz. the way patriarchy is structured in the political-economic base and the ideological superstructure. Hence, we must understand the content of this specific characteristic of ideological illusions for patriarchal oppression in great detail.

Patriarchy undeniably has its roots in the relations of production and reproduction.

As for the sexual division of labour of the primitive communist times, the following theoretical assumptions must be made. Naturally devoid of predatory characteristics such as sharp claws, great muscular strength and very fast running speed like tigers or cheetahs, the early human beings had to resort to living and working in groups to defend themselves from wild animals as well as for procuring necessary means of subsistence efficiently. As a result, the growth of population was an important factor for group-survival. As people would live in forests without concrete shelter and with little development of medicine, the infant and child mortality rate was understandably very high. As Gerda Lerner explains, skeletal studies estimate the average lifespan of people from the Paleolithic to the Neolithic age to be merely thirty to forty years. Besides, not only did mothers have to give birth to several children but also they had to spend the first 2-3 years of every child’s life in the latter’s nursing. Thus, for the survival of these primaeval groups, women would have to spend a lot of years in their adult lives in pregnancy, childbirth and childcare; and thus she theorizes that women must have themselves chosen those activities in social production which are easier to perform with constant pregnancy and childcare requirements, such as foraging and small-game hunting. Thus the very primaeval times of human lives had a division of labour based on sex differences, i.e. reproductive differences.

Further, as she stresses,

“These biological sex differences are not differences in the strength and endurance of men and women but solely reproductive differences, specifically women’s ability to nurse babies. Having said this, I want to stress that my acceptance of a ‘biological explanation’ holds only for the earliest stages of human development and does not mean that a later sexual division of labor based on women’s mothering is ‘natural’. On the contrary […] male dominance is a historic phenomenon in that it arose out of a biologically determined given situation and became a culturally created and enforced structure over time.”

This is an important reminder because humanity has long transcended from this primaeval stage; today most people do not live in forests, do not need to give birth to several children per pair, and women do not have to spend two to three years nursing one infant. Today, the sexual division of labour based on biological reproduction should not have a very significant role to play in the lives of women, and consequently women should not have to confine themselves to only a few activities in social production. That we still have women confined to only a few fields of paid social labour is not a necessary consequence of sexual division of social labour, but rather of the institutionalization of patriarchy intertwined with the development of class society, where the economic, sexual and reproductive exploitation as well as sociocultural oppression of women becomes an important factor to create and sustain class society.

As pointed out by Com. Frederick Engels in his Origin of the Family, Private Property and the State (1884),

“According to the materialistic conception, the determining factor in history is, in the final instance, the production and reproduction of the immediate essentials of life. This, again, is of a twofold character. On the one side, the production of the means of existence, of articles of food and clothing, dwellings, and of the tools necessary for that production; on the other side, the production of human beings themselves, the propagation of the species. The social organization under which the people of a particular historical epoch and a particular country live is determined by both kinds of production: by the stage of development of labor on the one hand and of the family on the other.”

As for the production of human beings or propagation of the species, the development of the institutions of family and marriage eventually led to male control on female sexual and reproductive capacities. This was initialized by several practices in the late primitive communist times (particularly in lower and middle barbarism), such as marriage by capture, marriage by purchase, marriage by rape; and in some regions a wedding custom whereby all men related to the bridegroom in the oldest to youngest order of age, would engage in sexual intercourse with the bride on her wedding night. Furthermore, the means of production and means of subsistence can not produce themselves — human labour is required for producing them. This is why in Com. Engels’ aforementioned quote, “the production of human beings themselves” is an important factor. Hence, men’s control on women’s sexual and reproductive capacities is first and foremost economic in nature.

As further elucidated by Com. Frederick Engels, the level of development of productive forces arose owing to the inception of cattle-rearing, metalworking, weaving and primitive agriculture that led to the birth of the social surplus, as a result of which prisoners taken in battles among tribes started to be kept as slaves. Continuing the age-old sexual division of labour, women would be in charge of the domestic sphere while men would be in charge of social production (now including the slaves). Moreover,

“In the old [primitive] communistic household, which comprised many couples and their children, the task entrusted to the women of managing the household was as much a public and socially necessary industry as the procuring of food by the men. With the patriarchal family, and still more with the single monogamous family, a change came. Household management lost its public character. It no longer concerned society. It became a private service; the wife became the head servant, excluded from all participation in social production.”

Thus the nature of domestic labour and consequently the nature of women’s labour became degraded, and women became economically subordinated to men.

At the same time, the emergence of private property, i.e. private ownership of instruments of production, led to a necessity for all of the family’s personal and private property to be passed from the father, the patriarch who owns all the instruments of social production, to his own children as the legitimate heirs. This was difficult in the then-prevailing system of matrilineal descent because all the private property was under the ownership of men who could not pass it on to their own children but only to their sisters’ children. Therefore, a change from matrilineal to patrilineal inheritance took place, whereby keeping everything else the same, only the right to own the instruments of social production would be passed on to children by their father instead of their mother. This introduction of the father-right also led to the creation of the monogamous family, which according to Com. Engels is,

“[...] based on the supremacy of the man, the express purpose being to produce children of undisputed paternity; such paternity is demanded because these children are later to come into their father’s property as his natural heirs.”

Further, he characterizes the monogamous family as

“the first form of the family to be based, not on natural, but on economic conditions — on the victory of private property over primitive, natural communal property.” [Italics by M.]

Thus, on one side, the monogamous family imposed strict standards of monogamy on the woman only, for economic reasons:

“Men compelled women to be faithful in marriage without recognizing a similar duty on their part. When a woman is faithless, she commits the worst deception to which a citizen of the new order can fall a victim; she brings another man’s children into his house to become the heirs of his property. That is why among all the ancient peoples adultery, when committed by a woman, was punishable by death or slavery.”

– August Bebel, Woman and Socialism [Italics by M.]

Meanwhile on the other side, the ongoing development of patriarchy also had a role in establishing the slave society — the first phase of class society in most regions of the world. After the material conditions arose for the creation of the social surplus,

“Increasing population and the desire for an extensive ownership of land for agricultural and pastoral purposes led to struggles and battles over the possession of such land; it also led to a demand for labor power. An increase in labor power meant greater wealth in produce and flock. To procure such labor power, the rape of women was at first resorted to, and then the enslavement of vanquished men who had formerly been killed.”

– August Bebel, Woman and Socialism [italics by M.]

As discussed above, there already were practices related to marriage in the lower and middle stages of barbarism that led to male control on female sexuality. With the emergence of private property and the necessity of slaveowning, along with the fact that men were the owners of all instruments of social labour including the slaves; the ruling classes, i.e. slaveowning men, exercised control on women’s sexual and reproductive capacities. As Gerda Lerner explains, slaves in general were dishonoured, and in the case of women slaves, raping them became the way to do so:

“Since their male kin had been slaughtered, these captives could have no hope of rescue or escape. Their isolation and hopelessness increased their captors’ sense of power. The process of dishonoring could in the case of women be combined with the final act of male dominance, the rape of captive women. If a woman had been captured with her children, she would submit to whatever condition her captors imposed in order to secure the survival of her children. If she had no children, her rape or sexual use would soon tend to make her pregnant, and experience would show the captors that women would endure enslavement and adapt to it in the hope of saving their children and eventually improving their lot.”

Ergo, we can conclude that patriarchy has its base in production relations whereby the ownership of the means of social production coming into the hands of men constitutes the roots of patriarchy, inseparable from the roots of class society. This is, of course, coupled with the fact that women were raped in order to be enslaved, constituted the first slaves, made up most of the slaves until the practice of enslaving men started later; and that the male control on women’s sexual and reproductive capacities still remains a fundamental feature of patriarchal oppression in class society.

Furthermore, in feudalism, whether women would be participating in domestic or social labour, they were not recognized as individual beings but only in accordance with their relations with the men in their family. As noted by comrades in CPI(ML) [PW], in the feudal society of India that started around 4th Century CE,

“A woman had no independent existence. She came to be identified either through her father, her husband or her son. Women of the tenant and bonded labourer classes had to accompany their husbands in toiling for the landlords. Thus women of the oppressed classes participated in social production.

Yet it must not be forgotten that they laboured only in the lands where their husbands toiled; or they served that family of landlords to which their husbands had been bonded. The woman was always an appendage to her husband. It was this patriarchal relation that mediated exploitation of her labour by the feudal classes. Women’s labour in social production was not recognised. It was thus not paid. [Woman’s] social labour was treated as an adjunct to that of her husband’s labour.” [Italics by M.]

In Europe too, where feudalism developed during the 5th to 7th Century CE, Com. Silvia Federici notes that despite there being several socioeconomic differences between landless, poor and rich peasants, women had a second-class status; not only were women excluded from the offices where the better-off peasants were employed, but also land was allotted usually to men and transmitted through the male lineage.

However, as for the servile peasants or serfs, she explains that serf-women were less dependent on serf-men and were not as subservient to men compared to modern proletarian women. Nevertheless, this was true only of relations within the family — the biggest authority for the serf-woman was the feudal lord, who was entitled to not just the land the serfs worked on but also their labour, marriage, remarriage, sexual behaviour as well as the jus primae noctis (right of the first night). In spite of all this,

“[...] since work on the servile farm was organized on a subsistence basis, the sexual division of labor in it was less pronounced and less discriminating than in a capitalist farm. In the feudal village no social separation existed between the production of goods and the reproduction of the work-force; all work contributed to the family's sustenance. Women worked in the fields, in addition to raising children, cooking, washing, spinning, and keeping an herb garden; their domestic activities were not devalued and did not involve different social relations from those of men, as they would later, in a money-economy, when housework would cease to be viewed as real work.”

– Silvia Federici, Caliban and the Witch.

In fact, she admits that women’s participation in the anti-feudal struggle was so strong that in later centuries, in order to undermine the class solidarity achieved in anti-feudal revolts, the state would have to come up with decriminalization of rape of poor working-class women (14th Century Venice and France) and mass witch hunts of peasant women (15th to 17th Century Europe – France, Germany, Switzerland, Italy, Europe, etc.). These coupled with state-sponsored prostitution and the recognition of prostitution as a “public service” by the Church (14th to 15th Century Italy and France) massively intensified the amount of misogyny.

As for the period of violent overthrow of feudalism by capitalism, Com. Federici clarifies that primitive accumulation comprised of settler colonialism and ethnic cleansing on the Turtle Island coupled with the slave plantations that subjugated the Africans, the looting of natural resources from the colonies, the conversion of the Commons to the Enclosures, the incarceration of vagabonds, as well as the witch-hunting of women. Particularly characteristic of this period was the overthrow of the feudal subsistence economy with the bourgeois private market-based economy, wherein the goal of the economy being commodity-production, reproductive labour became devalued; since the gendered division of labour since the primitive-communist times had already delegated all reproductive labour to women, women’s labour became devalued. As Com. Federici decodes,

“In the new monetary regime, only production-for-market was defined as a value-creating activity, whereas the reproduction of the worker began to be considered as valueless from an economic viewpoint and even ceased to be considered as work. Reproductive work continued to be paid — though at the lowest rates — when performed for the master class or outside the home. But the economic importance of the reproduction of labor-power carried out in the home, and its function in the accumulation of capital became invisible, being mystified as a natural vocation and labeled ‘women’s labor.’ In addition, women were excluded from many waged occupations and, when they worked for a wage, they earned a pittance compared to the average male wage.”

– Silvia Federici, Caliban and the Witch

In regions where the Industrial Revolution had not superseded the feudal economy in a drastic way (unlike England), reproductive labour was still valued. We can find an account of the families of the toiling masses producing a number of articles at home in order to be exchanged for others in the market in the absence of large-scale industrial production in Alexandra Kollontai’s description of the old, largely feudal society of 19th Century Russia,

“The woman did everything that the modern working and peasant woman has to do, but besides this cooking, washing, cleaning and mending, she spun wool and linen, wove cloth and garments, knitted stockings, made lace, prepared — as far as her resources permitted — all sorts of pickles, jams and other preserves for winter, and manufactured, her own candles.[…]

In our grandmother’s day, all this domestic work was necessary and beneficial; it ensured the well-being of the family. The more the mistress of the house applied herself, the better the peasant or craftsman’s family lived. Even the national economy benefited from the housewife’s activity, for the woman did not limit herself to making soup and cooking potatoes (i.e. satisfying the immediate needs of the family), she also produced such things as cloth, thread, butter, etc. which had a value as commodities that could be sold on the market.”

However, in regions where the Industrial Revolution completely overthrew the early-capitalist social relations, capitalists began to employ women and children while simultaneously paying them lower wages than those earned by the workingmen. The industrial revolution based modern capitalist economy, just like the slaveowning mode of production, was founded upon the labour of women. As Com. Angela Davis explains,

“New England’s textile mills were the factory system’s successful pioneers. Since spinning and weaving were traditional female domestic occupations, women were the first workers recruited by the mill-owners to operate the new power looms. Considering the subsequent exclusion of women from industrial production in general, it is one of the great ironies of this country’s economic history that the first industrial workers were women.”

Furthermore, as Com. Marx delineates in Capital,

“The value of labour-power was determined, not only by the labour-time necessary to maintain the individual adult labourer, but also by that necessary to maintain his family. Machinery, by throwing every member of that family on to the labour-market, spreads the value of the man’s labour-power over his whole family. It thus depreciates his labour-power. To purchase the labour-power of a family of four workers may, perhaps, cost more than it formerly did to purchase the labour-power of the head of the family, but, in return, four days’ labour takes the place of one, and their price falls in proportion to the excess of the surplus labour of four over the surplus labour of one. In order that the family may live, four people must now, not only labour, but expend surplus labour for the capitalist.”

And hence capitalism thrives on the exploitation of labour not just of an individual workingperson but rather on that of the whole proletarian family.

Even when more women than men are employed in social production, the lower wages received by workingwomen as compared to workingmen throw light on the patriarchal character of their economic exploitation. Besides, women are doubly oppressed:

(a) they have to engage in social labour and domestic labour both, as housework and childcare remain a part of women’s household duties with little to no help from men, and the few cases during the industrial revolution era where men became unemployed and started doing housework as their wives worked in industrial units still remain exceptions.

(b) the product and remuneration of women’s labour in social production is alienated not just by the capitalist class but also by the patriarch in their families. This is especially true for semi-feudal, semi-colonial societies like India about which I again quote comrades from CPI(ML) [PW]:

“Yet another feature of patriarchy in social production is the surrender of wages that women receive to the male head of the house. Hence there is a double alienation that women experience. Firstly as agricultural labourers and industrial workers the product is alienated from them by the landlord or the capitalist. Then their wage is alienated from them by the male head of the house. This practice simultaneously damages even that social consciousness which women develop out of their participation in social production. This deprivation of wages takes place in two different forms. One is the more brazen manner of forcing its surrender. The second is subtler. The male wage earner so manipulates the domestic situation that the woman’s income is spent on domestic needs and the male wage earner keeps the privilege of spending his money for his fads and to accumulate property or wealth. In short, his wage belongs to him while her wage belongs to the whole family.

Another important aspect concerning social production is that women have only had to toil but have not been allowed the right of owning property. Under feudalism all the means of production were owned by men of the feudal classes. Women of the feudal classes shared the fruits of toil of the peasantry, but were generally not allowed to enjoy rights over property. The forfeiture of property which commenced with the origin of private property has continued right into the feudal period as a law of class society. While capitalism does not legally deny a woman from owning property, in practice, the reality is that property is owned and transacted by the males. It is this dispossession of all forms of social wealth, which reinforces the act of depriving the woman of her wages too.” [Italics by M.]

As we can observe, women played an important role in the primaeval times in primitive communism through their reproductive labour, along with foraging and small-game hunting because it was not until the upper stage of savagery that the bow and arrow was invented and big-game hunting had commenced. The slaveowning mode of production was founded upon women’s labour as slaves in social, domestic, sexual and reproductive labour. The feudal system also had half of the means of subsistence being produced through women’s domestic labour as well as women’s unpaid labour in social production. The capitalist system’s primitive accumulation period was incomplete without separating reproductive from productive labour and devaluing the latter while its industrial revolution period was founded upon the labour of women and children, and still benefits a lot out of it — in both social production and reproduction. Basically, all stages of historical development of human society are founded upon and have gained immense growth through the labour of women. And yet, women’s labour has historically been coerced, unrecognized, unpaid and underpaid as a result of class society and patriarchy.

Moreover, it is important to note that in the case of patriarchal oppression, it is not just the domestic and social labour of women but also their sexual and reproductive labour which is exploited in all stages of class society. All societies require human labour for the purpose of production and circulation of the means of production and means of subsistence, but class society specifically requires a control on the sexual and reproductive capacities of females, not just for maintaining a patrilineal property inheritance but also for procreation to give birth to the next generation of workers and owners; and also standardizing domestic labour as women’s labour so that they raise children until the latter are ready to join the workforce, ensure a comfortable life for the workingmen in the family, etc. – basically to create and sustain labour-power for social production. The exploitation of sexual and reproductive capacities of women is thus an integral characteristic for the establishment and perpetuation of class society.

Furthermore, it is not uncommon to come across areas of social production today which are said to be “male-dominated” such as medicine, scientific research, engineering (especially mechanical and civil engineering), mathematics, mechanical repair, etc., and “female-dominated” areas such as nursing, babysitting, teaching, secretarial work, etc. wherein the latter are a cultural interpretation and extension of the historical necessity for women to nurse children in the primaeval epoch while the former are in part a result of the exclusion of women from higher education, especially the natural sciences and technology. As aptly noted by comrades in CPI(ML) [PW],

“The work done by women under capitalism is an extension of their domestic work. [...]

Even when women are employed on the production line in textile, food processing, pharmaceutical or electronics industries a sexual [sic] division of labour is preserved in the respective factories.”

Besides, one can observe a gendered division and exploitation of labour in, say cooking, with women being associated with the status of “cooks” or mere “housewives” whereas men are associated with the sophisticated status of “chefs”. Moreover, computer programming is also a field that historically employed low-paid women workers who were called “clerks” and “typists” instead of “programmers” by the men who owned these companies. Such degrading of the same type of work when done by women but glorifying when done by men is a patriarchal standard, and has nothing to do with the biological anatomy of males and females.

Some transgender and queer people have also been integrated in social production under capitalism, under:

“[...] ‘queer work’ — professions and industries where queer people helped each other get jobs, found some space to express their non-normative genders at work, reduced the risks of homophobic attack, and in some cases were favoured by employers. These jobs are those often associated with stereotyped gender roles of one’s opposite gender — specifically blue-collar labour for women, and activities like cooking, service, laundry, and food service for men [...] What makes these jobs queer is not really about sexual object choice, but about gender expectations. Employers, customers, and co-workers have assumptions about the gendered nature of the work itself, allowing gender nonconforming queer people to find a limited and difficult niche.”

– Michelle O’ Brian, Transgender Marxism

We must note, in both these cases, that the division of labour that is followed in today’s capitalist and semi-feudal semi-colonial societies is a gendered division of labour, and not a sexual division of labour. Gender is a sociocultural construct that maintains and reinforces the patriarchal oppression in class society, i.e. economic exploitation, control on sexual and reproductive capacities and sociocultural oppression of women (and resultantly of transgender and queer people too). Although the narrative around which the cultural construct of gender is created is that of biological sex, the real material roots of gender lie in the system of patriarchy whose own roots are of an economic nature; since (i) the role of women in childbearing, childrearing, domestic labour, etc. is primarily to create and sustain labour-power for Capital to exploit, and (ii) the patriarchal monogamous marriage based family’s aim is to preserve the right of men to pass down the family’s (personal and) private property to their own sons.

In primitive communist society when gender was not yet established, a sexual division of labour existed between males and females because it was important for the primaeval peoples to increase their population amidst difficult material conditions for reasons already listed before. Today, even though the control on women’s sexual and reproductive capacities remains central to the functioning of capitalist economy, the division of labour is more gendered and less sexual. For instance, if a cisgender woman is working as a nurse, that is not because there is some mysterious element in the vulva that makes females good nurses, but because of the sociocultural reason that affiliates occupations like nursing with femininity or womanhood. Hence, patriarchy operates in the material base by enforcing a gendered division of labour and a gendered exploitation of labour.

This can be further exemplified by how as mentioned before, women were identified under feudalism by their relationships with the men in their family: the husband for the married and the father for the unmarried woman. In one sense, it can be said that women are properties of their husbands or fathers. This can especially be interpreted by the feudal right of the first night, where the feudal lord establishes that he has the first right on every possession of his (male) serf, whereby the first experience of a serf-woman’s life after a serf-man marries her and thereby begins to own her, is that she is raped by the feudal lord who rules over her husband, the serf-man. Even today, the refusal of the state of India to recognize marital rape as rape is because even though all are supposedly equal in the eyes of the law, the wife still remains, figuratively, a property of her husband. Thus family and marital relations have their roots in the production relations of social and domestic production.

In the ideological superstructure, patriarchy prevails by enforcing strict gender roles through the social ideas of masculinity and femininity. We can take for an example, again, the gender pay gap between men and women in every field of social production today.

Com. Marx describes in Capital (Vol. 1),

“In so far as machinery dispenses with muscular power, it becomes a means of employing labourers of slight muscular strength, and those whose bodily development is incomplete, but whose limbs are all the more supple. The labour of women and children was, therefore, the first thing sought for by capitalists who used machinery. That mighty substitute for labour and labourers was forthwith changed into a means for increasing the number of wage-labourers by enrolling, under the direct sway of capital, every member of the workman’s family, without distinction of age or sex.”

Here as we have observed, women were not paid for their social labour under feudalism, and under capitalism, they were said to be brought to the workforce only because machinery became technologically advanced to the extent that even people with low muscular strength like women and children can operate them. This was used as a justification of lower wages of workingwomen compared to those of workingmen. The real motive, however, lay in the economic benefit received by the bourgeoisie as they employed four people (a whole family of workers) in the former wages of one worker. Today, too, the gender pay gap is justified by the social ideas of masculinity and femininity by asserting that women are only fit for domestic labour and that the fields under social production are not their area of expertise; and more so that women’s “natural” sphere lies in procreation, housework, and carework; while men who were the “hunters” are physically stronger than women and also have participation in social production as their “natural” sphere. From this follows, that the woman, confined to the household, is foolish; while the man, having exposure and knowledge of the world outside, is intelligent. Hence no matter whether an occupation requires material or mental labour, the woman’s lower wages are justified because according to patriarchy and class society, men are said to be strong and intelligent whereas women are said to be weak and foolish.

The real reason for the gender pay gap, however, does not lie in the differences of physical strength or intellectual capabilities between men and women, but rather in the ideological affixing and equalization of women’s labour with (the restricted and private-natured) domestic labour under capitalism. As noted earlier, domestic labour lost its public character with the onset of private property and since women were in charge of the same, their social status degraded too. Under class Society, in slavery the slaves did not have an opportunity to live with their families, but in feudalism there was a patriarchal family for serfs, peasants, artisans and all other working masses. In feudalism, the family was both a unit of production and consumption; as not only was all social production of serfs, peasants and artisans done by the family as a unit, but also a number of articles would be produced by women in the feudal-patriarchal family up until the pre-industrial revolution times for consumption and exchange. Moreover, the epoch of primitive accumulation witnessed not just Enclosures and slave plantations but also decriminalization of rape of proletarian women, epidemics in Europe leading to a population crisis which was “handled” by the states’ initiatives for population growth (i.e. penalties on abortion and contraception for women, thus state-control of women’s reproductive autonomy), mass witch-hunts (arguably a way to ensure the former), as well as a political campaign by journeymen to exclude working craftswomen from workshops.

The industrial revolution, however, not only employed a large number of women and children, but also led to large-scale production of articles produced in the home-based economy of feudalism and their sales at affordable prices. As Com. Angela Davis elucidates,

“While home-manufactured goods were valuable primarily because they fulfilled basic family needs, the importance of factory-produced commodities resided overwhelmingly in their exchange value — in their ability to fulfill employers’ demands for profit. This revaluation of economic production revealed — beyond the physical separation of home and factory — a fundamental structural separation between the domestic home economy and the profit-oriented economy of capitalism. Since housework does not generate profit, domestic labour was naturally defined as an inferior form of work as compared to capitalist wage labour.

An important ideological by-product of this radical economic transformation was the birth of the ‘housewife’. Women began to be ideologically redefined as the guardians of a devalued domestic life.”

Furthermore, even though in reality there were numerous immigrant proletarian women to counter this myth and only middle-class families could afford to have housewives who do not actively earn a wage, bourgeois propaganda equated the housewife with the woman and vice versa.

“Although the ‘housewife’ was rooted in the social conditions of the bourgeoisie and the middle classes, nineteenth-century ideology established the housewife and the mother as universal models of womanhood. Since popular propaganda represented the vocation of all women as a function of their roles in the home, women compelled to work for wages came to be treated as alien visitors within the masculine world of the public economy. Having stepped outside their ‘natural’ sphere, women were not to be treated as full-fledged wage workers. The price they paid involved long hours, substandard working conditions and grossly inadequate wages.”

– Angela Davis, Women, Race and Class [Italics by M.]

As Com. Federici maintains,

“These historic changes — that peaked in the 19th century with the creation of the full-time housewife — redefined women’s position in society and in relation to men. The sexual division of labor that emerged from it not only fixed women to reproductive work, but increased their dependence on men, enabling the state and employers to use the male wage as a means to command women’s labor. In this way, the separation of commodity production from the reproduction of labor-power also made possible the development of a specifically capitalist use of the wage and of the markets as means for the accumulation of unpaid labor.”

– Silvia Federici, Caliban and the Witch [Italics by M.]

She also connects the formation of the ideological conception of a “housewife” with the banning of prostitutes, along with the exclusion of women from waged work. As prostituted women would perform domestic labour like washing and cooking for the customer-men along with sexual labour, the number of prostitutes massively widened in the wake of land privatization and commercialization of agriculture that led to the expulsion of peasant women from agricultural lands during the 16th to 17th Century. However, the establishment of capitalism required a bourgeois and state control on women’s bodily autonomy as well as required labour discipline via the institution of the family. This is why, women became excluded from all kinds of waged work as well as prostitution (where prostitutes were then criminalized), whereas reproductive work became to be considered as women’s “natural vocation” wherein:

“[...] in pre-capitalist Europe women’s subordination to men had been tempered by the fact that they had access to the commons and other communal assets, while in the new capitalist regime women themselves became the commons, as their work was defined as a natural resource, laying outside the sphere of market relations.”

– Silvia Federici, Caliban and the Witch.

However, the popular conception connects women’s unpaid reproductive labour (including social reproduction) to “mother’s love”, “wife’s love”, “servitude as a virtue of the woman”, etc. — social ideas around femininity — while keeping in the dark the massive witch-allegations (and rapes and murders of alleged witches) that led to such a social order where disciplining women is a necessity for the mode of production. Therefore, masculinity and femininity are ideological illusions having their basis in the production relations of class society, whose history is intertwined with the history of patriarchy. Simultaneously, patriarchal oppression has developed in the exact way it did and to the exact extent it has owing to the development and predominance of class society and the former’s usefulness in establishing and maintaining class society.
